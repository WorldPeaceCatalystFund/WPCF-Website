Systemic Social Mechanism Diagram



The purpose of this report is to show the narrow focus of current political rhetoric when countries start to engage in hostile relations and the one-way direction in which the conversation is structured. Examples of countries engaging in hostile relations include Russia-Ukraine, Taiwan-China, IsraelPalestine, Armenia-Azerbaijan, etc. There is a general form of the conversation formed between any two hostilities. I want to show that knowledge of the social bonds of society allows us to break this cycle.

When two countries start to engage in hostile relations, there is a standard form of conversation and a quick escalation thereof. The standard form of conversation goes something like this: One country wants something from the other, usually land, and the other country resists. There is a one-way direction of the conversation and that is war or the escalation of using non-conventional weapons. This is a conversation well. I furnish specific details in the case study on Russia-Ukraine.

The reason there is a standard structure is mob mentality. Politicians successfully use their rhetoric to excite the masses. Once a mass group of people starts to think similarly, it becomes a mob mentality, which is hard to stop. War is the inevitable result and is supported through a mob mentality. This is the way mobs go unless they have pathways to think beyond the prevalent political rhetoric. These pathways are the social bonds of society known by the public.

If two countries socialize with each other enough, then they know enough about what they like and what they don’t and know how to engage peacefully with each other. This knowledge is called the social bonds of society. This knowledge does not guarantee aversion to war but provides counter thoughts and arguments to hostile political rhetoric politicians try and push forth. The way it would manifest in reality is that the countries could not fight each other because they were so many people on either side who were friends with those on the other side.

This is social vulcanization. It is similar to the vulcanization procedure used to produce rubber tires. If you are unfamiliar, vulcanization is the process of adding sulfur to the rubber mixture which causes cross-linking and gives rubber its consistency and ductility. If enough people from two countries were friends with each other, they could not go to war with each other. There would be too many social cross-links which result in a ‘bricking’. Politicians could not successfully get their people to engage in warfare because of the social consistency.

This goes back to the battle of good vs evil. People can choose which side they want to be on, pro-war or pro-peace. The knowledge of the social bonds of society would need to be held and enacted by the good people such that it becomes the dominant force and war is averted. This is the natural law of bigger people playing law keepers. This is how war is averted. When there is a critical number of good people enacting the social bonds of society, two countries cannot engage in warfare because they are friends with each other.

In conclusion, there is a standard structure of conversation between two hostile nations that has a oneway direction toward violence. This standard structure, I dub a conversation a well, as it is a deep hole. The political use of mob mentality makes it easy to start wars. Knowledge of the social bonds of society allows the masses to establish pathways out of mob mentality which bricks a politician’s effort to start a war. Evidence of this is seen in the protests and sabotage done by Russian soldiers in the Russia-Ukraine war.
